Klemens Strasser is an indie developer from Graz, Austria. He makes apps like *The Art of Fauna*, *PocketShelf*, *Letter Rooms*, and *Study Snacks*. He won an Apple Design Award in 2025 for accessibility and was a finalist in 2022 and 2023.

I caught his talk and chatted with him afterward. Here's what stuck with me.

<Figure src={stageImg} alt="Klemens Strasser presenting on stage" caption="Klemens on stage talking about accessibility in indie apps." />

## Accessibility is for everyone, not just the obvious cases

Most developers think of accessibility as VoiceOver support or dynamic type. Klemens goes further. In *The Art of Fauna*, he added phobia filters for snakes, insects, and other animals. Some users don't even know they have these phobias until they see the content. Putting that setting on the first screen means they never have to.

He also makes the case that accessibility settings should come before onboarding, not after. Some users literally can't finish onboarding without them.

## Show users you're a real person

Klemens puts a photo of himself in the "contact the developer" section. When users are frustrated about a subscription, he explains why the app costs money and why he doesn't run ads (it would defeat the purpose of the app). The idea is simple: meet users at their point of frustration and show you actually care.

He also times the "Rate this app?" prompt right after the user finishes a hard puzzle. Catch people when they feel good.

## The two question filter

Every idea goes through two questions: Is this fun? Can I make it accessible? If the second answer is no, he passes.

## How he validates ideas

I asked him how he knows an idea is worth building. He said he prototypes it first, and if it makes him smile when he plays it, he shows it to friends. If they don't get it, he drops it. If they do, he keeps going. No overthinking it.

## Not every app needs to solve a problem

This one surprised me. *PocketShelf* started from a personal need (tracking what he reads), but *The Art of Fauna* started as pure fun. Users later told him it helped them relax, and that became part of what the app is. The purpose came after, not before.

I've been reading *The Cold Start Problem* by Andrew Chen, and the "come for the tool, stay for the network" concept made me curious whether he'd ever add social features. He said maybe for *Art of Fauna*, but it's not a priority. Which kind of proves the point: the tool has to be good on its own first.

---

As someone who keeps feeling stuck on what to build next, the biggest takeaway is just: make something that makes you smile, put it in front of people, and let the purpose reveal itself.
